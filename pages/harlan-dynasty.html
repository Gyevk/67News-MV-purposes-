The Alfonzo Dynasty: How Three Generations of a Political Family Ran a Province Through Corruption

A 67News investigation reveals that Jacob's crimes are not an anomaly — they are the latest chapter in a decades-long pattern of abuse, cover-ups, and unchecked power that the Alfonzo family has wielded over this province since the 1990s.

IL
Investigative Team — Irene Lim & David Park
67News Investigations Unit
Saturday, April 26, 2026  ·  12 min read
"This family didn't just hold power — they hoarded it, protected it, and passed it down like an inheritance. The corruption was the inheritance." — Former provincial auditor, speaking anonymously

Jacob Alfonzo's guilty verdict shocked the public. But for those who have watched this province's politics closely for decades, it was not a surprise. It was an inevitability.

The Alfonzo family has governed this province — directly or indirectly — for over thirty years. Three members of the family have held the governorship. And according to a 67News investigation drawing on court documents, financial records, and interviews with more than twenty current and former officials, corruption has been a constant thread running through every one of those administrations.

The First Alfonzo: Harold Sr.

Harold Alfonzo Sr. first took office in 1993, running on a platform of civic renewal and anti-poverty investment. Within two years, provincial auditors flagged irregularities in the public housing budget — funds meant to build affordable homes were instead routed to contractors with ties to the Alfonzo family's private business interests.

An internal investigation was opened in 1996. It was quietly closed in 1997 after the lead auditor was reassigned and two key witnesses recanted their statements. No charges were ever filed. Harold Sr. served two full terms and left office in 2001 with a 68% approval rating.

The Second Alfonzo: Harold Jr.

Harold Alfonzo Jr. succeeded his father in 2005 after a landslide election. His administration was marked by aggressive infrastructure spending — and, investigators now believe, equally aggressive skimming of those budgets.

Three former procurement officers, speaking to 67News on condition of anonymity, described a system in which contractors were required to pay a percentage of their contract value — typically between 8% and 12% — into accounts controlled by Alfonzo associates. "It was just the cost of doing business with the province," one former officer said. "Everyone knew. Nobody talked."

Harold Jr. resigned in 2011 citing health reasons. An Ombudsman inquiry was opened the following year but was ultimately dropped due to insufficient evidence — evidence that, sources say, had been systematically destroyed.

Richard Alfonzo: The Third Term

Richard Alfonzo — Jacob's father — took office in 2015, presenting himself as a reformer who would clean up the province's reputation. He won re-election twice. And while his administration has been more careful than his predecessors, the pattern, investigators say, never truly stopped.

"The methods got more sophisticated," said one former NBI investigator. "Shell companies, intermediaries, unofficial advisors. Jacob was the next generation of that system. He just got caught."

30+ years of Alfonzo rule

A System Built to Protect Itself

What makes the Alfonzo story remarkable is not just the scale of the alleged corruption — it is the durability of it. For three decades, investigations were opened and closed. Witnesses recanted. Auditors were reassigned. Evidence disappeared.

"This wasn't accidental," said Dr. Marcus Webb, a political scientist who has studied the province's governance for fifteen years. "This was a system. A machine. And it was very, very good at protecting itself."

Jacob's conviction may be the first crack in that machine. With formal legislative inquiries now underway and Ombudsman investigators reportedly reviewing the family's financial history going back to the 1990s, the full scope of what the Alfonzo dynasty built — and what it cost this province — may finally be coming to light.

For the residents who lived through it — who watched their public housing crumble, their roads go unrepaired, their votes get bought — the verdict is not justice. Not yet. But it may be the beginning of it.
