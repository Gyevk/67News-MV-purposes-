'No Comment' to the Press, 'Guilty' to the Judge: Jacob Alfonzo's Wall of Silence Finally Breaks in Court

For months Jacob Alfonzo refused every interview request, ignored every journalist, and said nothing publicly. Then he walked into a courtroom and said the one word everyone had been waiting for.

ML
Margaret Lowe
Senior Political Correspondent
Sunday, April 27, 2026  ·  6 min read
"Guilty, Your Honor." — Jacob Alfonzo, in court Sunday morning

He turned away every camera, declined every call, and had his lawyers issue a blanket statement refusing all media contact. But when it mattered most — standing before a Sandiganbayan justice with the weight of five criminal charges against him — Jacob Alfonzo spoke clearly and without hesitation: guilty.

Months of Silence

From the moment he was taken into custody in April, Jacob Alfonzo said nothing. His legal team issued a single public statement — "Mr. Alfonzo maintains his innocence and will address these matters through the appropriate legal channels" — and then went dark.

Reporters camped outside his residence. Interview requests were sent through three separate legal representatives. A major national broadcaster offered a prime-time slot with full editorial control. All were declined. All were met with silence.

When approached outside the Sandiganbayan during a preliminary hearing, Jacob walked past a row of journalists without breaking stride, eyes forward, expression unreadable. It became something of a symbol — the man at the center of the province's biggest political scandal, utterly unreachable.

The Plea

Sunday's session was listed on the court docket as a routine status hearing. No press were tipped off. No statement was issued in advance. It was only when court records were updated mid-morning that journalists realized what had happened.

Jacob Alfonzo had entered a guilty plea on all five counts — embezzlement, bribery, vote buying, obstruction of a COA audit, and unlawful interference with public procurement. The plea was entered quietly, in a courtroom with fewer than a dozen people present.

His attorney, in a brief statement afterward, said only that Jacob "accepts responsibility for his actions and asks for the opportunity to make amends." No further elaboration was provided. No interview was granted.

What the Silence Means

Legal analysts say the decision to stay silent with the press while pleading guilty in court is a calculated one. "By saying nothing publicly, he gives nothing to the opposition, nothing to the media cycle, and nothing that can be used against him at sentencing," said criminal defense attorney Priya Nair. "The guilty plea, paradoxically, is the safest move. It ends the trial, limits the damage, and lets him begin the process of rebuilding — eventually."

Others see it differently. "He had a chance to speak to the people of this province," said civic advocate Rosa Mendez. "To explain himself. To show some remorse. He chose not to. That tells you everything about how this family views the public — as an audience, not as people they owe anything to."

A Decade in the Making

It is worth remembering how this story began. Ten years ago, in April 2016, Governor Richard Alfonzo stood before cameras and introduced Jacob to the world — a son kept secret for decades, presented as the next chapter of the Alfonzo legacy. The province watched. Some were moved. Many were skeptical.

Jacob went on to enter politics, win his seat in 2023, and serve three years in office. And now, in the same month his father first revealed his existence to the public a decade ago, Jacob Alfonzo has pleaded guilty to five counts of corruption in a near-empty courtroom — without a word to the people he was elected to serve.

The Governor's Silence

Governor Richard Alfonzo has not made a public appearance since the plea was entered. His office confirmed he was informed of the decision in advance but offered no comment beyond a brief written statement: "This is a painful day for our family. We ask for privacy as we process this."

The provincial legislature, meanwhile, is not waiting. A formal inquiry into the governor's own conduct is now underway, with the first hearings scheduled for next month. Whether Richard Alfonzo will speak — to investigators, to the press, or to the public — remains to be seen.

For now, the Alfonzo family's defining characteristic holds: silence. But the questions are getting louder, and the walls are getting thinner.
