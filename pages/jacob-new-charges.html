It Gets Worse: Ombudsman Files Seven New Corruption Charges Against Jacob Alfonzo

Just as sentencing was approaching, investigators uncovered a second layer of criminal conduct — ghost employees, illegal land deals, and a ₱400 million ghost project that never existed on paper or on the ground.

RC
Raymond Cruz
Courts & Justice Correspondent
Wednesday, May 7, 2026  ·  8 min read
"What we found in the second audit was not a continuation of the first scheme. It was an entirely separate operation — running parallel, hidden deeper, and in some ways more brazen." — Senior Ombudsman investigator, speaking on background

The Office of the Ombudsman filed seven new criminal charges against Jacob Alfonzo on Wednesday, just weeks before his scheduled sentencing — revealing a second, previously undiscovered layer of corruption that investigators say dwarfs the offenses he has already pleaded guilty to.

The new charges, filed before the Sandiganbayan on Wednesday morning, stem from a second COA audit triggered by the original investigation. Investigators say that while reviewing financial records tied to Jacob's office, they discovered a parallel network of fraudulent transactions that had been carefully concealed behind legitimate government expenditures.

"The first case was the tip of the iceberg," said one senior investigator who spoke on background. "What we found underneath was significantly larger and significantly more deliberate."

The New Charges in Detail

Ghost Employees (3 counts): Investigators found that Jacob's office had been paying salaries to at least 47 individuals who were never employed by the provincial government. The ghost payroll, running for an estimated 26 months, diverted approximately ₱89 million in public funds into accounts traced back to Alfonzo associates. Several of the fictitious employees were listed under the names of deceased individuals.

Illegal Land Acquisition (2 counts): Documents show that Jacob used his position to facilitate the transfer of two parcels of government-owned land — valued at a combined ₱312 million — to private entities at a fraction of their assessed value. One parcel, a 4.2-hectare lot originally designated for a public school, was sold to a shell corporation for ₱1.8 million. The corporation has since been linked to a relative of Jacob's chief of staff.

Ghost Infrastructure Project (1 count): Perhaps the most audacious charge — investigators allege that Jacob approved and disbursed ₱400 million for a provincial road widening project that was never built. The project appeared in official budget documents, had a signed contractor agreement, and even generated progress reports. But field inspections found no evidence the work was ever started. The contractor listed on the agreement does not exist as a registered business entity.

Malversation of Calamity Funds (1 count): Following a typhoon that struck the province in late 2024, Jacob's office received ₱75 million in national disaster relief funds. Investigators allege that less than 30% of those funds reached affected communities. The remainder was disbursed to suppliers who provided no goods or services, with payments processed within days of the funds' arrival — an unusually fast turnaround that auditors flagged as a red flag for pre-arranged fraud.

Key Facts

7 new charges filed
₱876M total in new allegations
12 total charges across both cases

Sentencing Now Delayed

The filing of new charges has thrown Jacob's sentencing into uncertainty. His legal team immediately filed a motion requesting that the May 14 sentencing date be postponed pending resolution of the new case. The Sandiganbayan has yet to rule on the motion.

Legal analysts say the new charges significantly complicate Jacob's position. "He pleaded guilty to the first set of charges, which was already a calculated move to limit exposure," said criminal law professor Atty. Marco Santos. "But now there's a second case. The calculus has completely changed. He can't plead his way out of this one as cleanly."

Political Fallout

The new charges have intensified pressure on Governor Richard Alfonzo, who is already under a separate legislative inquiry. Opposition lawmakers called an emergency session Wednesday afternoon, with several members calling for the governor's immediate suspension pending the outcome of both investigations.

"The ghost project alone — ₱400 million for a road that was never built — happened on this governor's watch," said Provincial Board Member Carla Reyes. "Either he knew, or he was so detached from his own province's finances that he is unfit to hold office. Either way, he cannot continue."

Governor Alfonzo's office did not respond to requests for comment by the time of publication.

The Bigger Picture

Taken together, the two cases against Jacob Alfonzo now involve twelve criminal charges and alleged losses to the public of over ₱1.1 billion — a figure that, investigators note, covers only what has been documented so far. The COA audit of Jacob's office is ongoing, and sources within the bureau say additional irregularities are still being reviewed.

"We are not done," said one auditor who asked not to be named. "What has been filed is what we can prove right now. There is more."

For the people of this province — who watched their roads crumble, their calamity funds disappear, and their votes get bought — the expanding case is both a reckoning and a reminder. The Alfonzo name, once synonymous with political power, is now synonymous with something else entirely.
